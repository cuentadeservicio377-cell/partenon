# Buffer — Company Card

**Business type:** Bootstrapped SaaS / social media management platform
**Location:** Fully remote; team distributed across 15+ countries; founders originally from the UK
**Founded:** 2010
**Approximate size:** 73 employees (2026 estimate); ~69,000 paying customers
**Website:** https://buffer.com

## Services and products

- Social media scheduling, publishing, and analytics across LinkedIn, X/Twitter, Instagram, Facebook, Pinterest, TikTok, Mastodon, Threads, Bluesky, Google Business Profile, and YouTube Shorts
- Browser extension and mobile apps for iOS and Android
- Team collaboration features with draft approvals and custom access levels
- Free tier limited to 3 channels and 10 scheduled posts per channel
- Paid Essentials and Team plans billed per channel per month

## Rough revenue estimate

Buffer publishes its financials transparently. Public reporting as of 2026 indicates:

- **~$23M ARR**
- **~69,000 paying customers**
- **$202,459 net profit in 2024** (distributed as 15% profit-share across the team)
- **~$2M projected net profit on ~$23M ARR for 2025**

Buffer bought back investor equity in 2018 and has operated as an independent, profitable, bootstrapped company since then.

## Real pain points from public sources

1. **Competition in a crowded market.** Buffer competes with Hootsuite, Sprout Social, Later, and newer entrants. ProductGrowth's 2026 review notes that Buffer's analytics depth is lighter than enterprise competitors and that it lacks native WhatsApp Business scheduling, which is critical for some markets.
2. **Pricing and procurement friction.** The same review flags that Buffer bills only in USD, offers no GST invoice for Indian buyers, and lacks India-domestic entity support, which can complicate procurement for international SMBs.
3. **Feature prioritization under constraints.** As a bootstrapped company, Buffer must choose which platforms and features to support. The Threads integration, for example, shipped after 7,000+ community requests, illustrating how customer demand queues compete for limited engineering resources.
4. **Transparency trade-offs.** Buffer was an early pioneer of radical transparency (public salaries, revenue, and equity). The Bootstrapped Founder noted in 2020 that Buffer later closed its public revenue dashboard, observing that public data can become a competitive disadvantage or paint a distorting picture as companies mature.
