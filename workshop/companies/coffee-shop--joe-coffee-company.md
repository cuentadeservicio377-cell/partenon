# Company Card: Joe Coffee Company

**Business type:** Coffee shop / specialty coffee roaster  
**Location:** Headquarters at 131 West 21st Street, New York, NY 10011, USA; 20+ cafes in New York City and Philadelphia  
**Founded:** 2003  
**Approximate size:** 80–175 employees (estimates vary by source); 20+ retail locations plus a Long Island City roastery  
**Website:** https://joecoffeecompany.com

---

## Services and products

- Specialty espresso drinks, pour-over, cold brew, and seasonal beverages
- In-house roasted whole-bean coffee sold retail and wholesale
- Catering, private events, and mobile-ordering via the Joe Coffee app
- Barista training and coffee education classes
- Licensing and wholesale relationships with offices, hotels, and grocers

---

## Rough revenue estimate

Public data sources estimate revenue between **$25M and $119M annually**, depending on methodology. RocketReach reports ~$119M and ~175 employees, while Owler and ZoomInfo show smaller ranges. Specialty coffee chains of this scale typically operate on thin gross margins after rent, labor, and green-coffee costs.

---

## Real pain points (from public sources)

1. **High fixed costs in prime NYC real estate.** Joe Coffee’s original Flatiron location simultaneously served as a cafe, training center, and makeshift headquarters until SquareFoot helped relocate the company to a dedicated office because the team was “bursting at the seams.”
2. **Labor scheduling and retention pressure.** Glassdoor data for Joe Coffee in New York City shows compensation-and-benefits ratings around 2.7–3.1 out of 5, suggesting payroll and retention are ongoing concerns in a high-cost city.
3. **Supply chain volatility.** As a roaster-retailer, Joe Coffee depends on green-coffee sourcing, freight, and packaging; even small commodity or shipping shocks compress margins.
4. **Multi-location coordination.** With 20+ cafes, daily tasks—inventory, scheduling, waste tracking, and local marketing—are hard to standardize without an operations system.
5. **Catering and wholesale follow-up.** Corporate catering and licensing deals require quotes, invoices, and reminders; without automation, accounts can slip through the cracks.
